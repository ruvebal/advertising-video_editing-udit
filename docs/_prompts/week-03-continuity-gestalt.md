### Core Lecture: Continuity Editing Theory (45 minutes)

#### 1. The Birth of Continuity (15 min)

**Historical Context:**
Early filmmakers discovered that cutting between shots could be confusing unless certain rules were followed.

**D.W. Griffith's Innovations:**
- **Cross-cutting (Parallel Editing):** Show two events happening simultaneously in different locations
- **Close-ups:** Move camera closer for emotional emphasis
- **Shot variety:** Wide, medium, close-up within same scene
- **Eyeline matches:** Character looks off-screen, cut to what they see

**The Goal: Invisible Editing**
Make cuts so smooth that viewers don't notice themâ€”they're absorbed in the story.

**Key Principles Established:**

**1. The 180-Degree Rule (The Line)**
- Imagine a line between two characters in conversation
- Keep camera on ONE SIDE of that line
- If you cross the line, characters appear to flip positions
- Result: Spatial confusion

**Demonstrate with diagram on board:**
```
    [Character A]  â†â†’  [Character B]
         â†‘                    â†‘
    Camera Side 1        Camera Side 1
    (Stay on this side)
```

**Show examples:**
- Correct: Classic dialogue scene maintaining the line
- Incorrect: Scene that crosses the line (characters flip)

**2. Match on Action**
- Cut during movement (character stands, opens door, picks up object)
- Motion masks the cut
- Brain focuses on action, not the edit

**3. Eyeline Match**
- Character looks off-screen (establish direction)
- Cut to what they're looking at (from their POV or near it)
- Creates spatial relationship

**4. Shot-Reverse-Shot**
- Standard dialogue coverage
- Over-the-shoulder of Character A (showing B)
- Reverse: Over-the-shoulder of Character B (showing A)
- Maintains screen direction and spatial coherence

> *"The 180-degree rule is not a prisonâ€”it is a foundation. Master it before you break it."*

#### 2. Gestalt Psychology and Film Perception (20 min)

**What is Gestalt Psychology?**
German school of psychology (1920s) studying how humans perceive patterns and wholes.

**Key Principle:** "The whole is greater than the sum of its parts."

**Gestalt Laws Applied to Editing:**

**1. Law of Closure**
- Humans mentally complete incomplete patterns
- In film: We fill gaps between shots
- Example: Character reaches for doorknob (cut) Door opens
  - We don't need to see the hand turning the knobâ€”our brain fills it in

**2. Law of Continuity**
- We perceive continuous forms even when interrupted
- In film: Movement or direction continues across cuts
- Example: Ball thrown (cut) Ball caught
  - We perceive continuous trajectory even though we didn't see the middle

**3. Law of Proximity**
- Objects close together are perceived as related
- In film: Shots in sequence are perceived as connected
- Example: Shot of person looking worried (cut) Shot of approaching storm
  - We assume they're related in time/space

**4. Law of Similarity**
- Similar elements are grouped together
- In film: Similar shots (same location, lighting, characters) feel continuous
- Example: Multiple shots in same room with same lighting = one continuous scene

**Why This Matters for Editors:**
- **Continuity editing exploits these natural tendencies**
- Our brains WANT to see continuityâ€”we work to make sense of cuts
- Editors can rely on viewers to "fill in" missing information
- This allows for economy of storytelling (don't show everything)

**Application to Advertising:**
- Clear spatial relationships = clear message
- Viewers shouldn't be confused about what's happening
- Continuity editing ensures product/message is understood
- Exception: Intentional confusion for artistic effect

> *"The editor does not create continuity. The editor reveals the continuity that the viewer's mind creates."*

#### 3. When to Break the Rules (10 min)

**Continuity rules are guidelines, not laws.**

**Reasons to Break Continuity:**

**1. Disorientation (Intentional)**
- Horror films: Break spatial logic to create unease
- Psychological thrillers: Confuse viewer to match character's confusion

**2. Energy and Pace**
- Action sequences: Rapid cuts, sometimes crossing the line for intensity
- Music videos: Continuity less important than rhythm

**3. Stylistic Choice**
- French New Wave: Jump cuts as aesthetic
- Modern advertising: Fast, chaotic cuts for youth energy

**4. Impossible Spaces**
- Surreal advertising: Deliberately break spatial logic
- Example: Character walks through door, emerges in different location

**The Key:** Know the rules so you can break them intentionally, not accidentally.

**Discussion:** "Can you think of ads that break continuity? Why did they do it?"

### Technical Workshop: Multi-Camera Editing (20 minutes)

#### Adobe Premiere Pro: Multi-Camera Workflow

**Method 1: Traditional (Manual Assembly)**

**1. Organize Footage:**
- Create bins for each angle: WIDE, MEDIUM_A, MEDIUM_B, CLOSEUP_A, CLOSEUP_B
- Name clips clearly

**2. Build Sequence:**
- Start with wide shot (establishes space)
- Cut to medium shots for dialogue
- Cut to close-ups for emotional moments
- Use shot-reverse-shot pattern

**3. Trimming Tools:**
- **Ripple Edit (B):** Trim and close gap automatically
- **Rolling Edit (N):** Adjust cut point between two clips
- **Slip Tool (Y):** Change content without changing duration

**Method 2: Multi-Camera Editing (Advanced)**

**If you shot with multiple cameras simultaneously:**

```
1. Select all clips from same take
2. Right-click > Create Multi-Camera Source Sequence
3. Sync by: Audio, Timecode, or In Points
4. Edit: Switch between angles in real-time
5. Refine: Adjust cuts after initial pass
```

**Pro Tip:** Even if you didn't shoot multi-cam, you can fake it by syncing takes manually.

#### Audio Transitions: J-Cuts and L-Cuts

**J-Cut (Audio Leads Video):**
- Hear next scene before seeing it
- Creates smooth transition
- Example: Hear Character B's response before cutting to them

**How to create:**
1. Unlink audio and video (Right-click > Unlink)
2. Extend audio track earlier than video
3. Creates "J" shape on timeline

**L-Cut (Video Leads Audio):**
- See next scene while hearing previous
- Natural conversation flow
- Example: Cut to Character B while Character A still speaking

**How to create:**
1. Unlink audio and video
2. Extend audio track later than video
3. Creates "L" shape on timeline

**Demonstrate both techniques live.**

> *"The J-cut and L-cut are the editor's secret weapons. They make dialogue feel like real conversation, not edited clips."*

### Practical Exercise: Film and Edit Continuity Scene (70 minutes)

#### Exercise Overview

**Objective:** Film and edit a 30-second dialogue scene with perfect continuity.

**Scenario Options (Choose one or create your own):**

**Option 1: The Pitch**
- Character A pitches an idea/product to Character B
- B is skeptical, asks questions
- A convinces B (or doesn't)

**Option 2: The Confession**
- Character A reveals something to Character B
- B reacts with surprise/concern
- Resolution or cliffhanger

**Option 3: The Argument**
- Characters disagree about something
- Tension builds
- Ends with agreement or walking away

#### Production Phase (35 minutes)

**Setup (10 minutes):**
- Pairs find filming location
- Set up simple background (wall, desk, outdoor space)
- Establish the "line" (mark with tape if helpful)
- Plan camera positions (all on one side of line)

**Coverage to Shoot:**

**1. Wide Shot (Establishing)**
- Both characters in frame
- Shows spatial relationship
- Shoot entire scene in one take

**2. Medium Shots (Over-the-Shoulder)**
- Over A's shoulder, showing B
- Over B's shoulder, showing A
- Shoot entire scene from each angle

**3. Close-Ups (Optional but recommended)**
- Close-up of A (for emotional moments)
- Close-up of B (for reactions)
- Shoot key lines/reactions

**Filming Tips:**

**1. Consistent Performance:**
- Actors should repeat dialogue/actions similarly each take
- Makes editing easier (matching action)

**2. Overlap Dialogue:**
- Start recording before scene begins
- Keep recording after scene ends
- Gives editing flexibility

**3. Maintain Eyelines:**
- Characters should look at same spot (where other actor is)
- Even when other actor isn't in frame

**4. Audio Continuity:**
- Consistent volume and tone
- Minimize background noise changes

**Instructor Role:**
- Circulate and check camera positions
- Ensure students are maintaining the 180-degree rule
- Help troubleshoot technical issues

#### Editing Phase (30 minutes)

**Import and Organize:**
1. Transfer footage to computers
2. Create bins for each angle
3. Review all footage, note best takes

**Assembly:**

**Step 1: Lay Down Wide Shot (5 min)**
- Place wide shot on timeline
- This is your "safety" coverage
- Trim to best 30 seconds

**Step 2: Add Medium Shots (10 min)**
- Cut to medium of Character A when they speak
- Cut to medium of Character B when they speak
- Use shot-reverse-shot pattern
- Cut on action or during pauses

**Step 3: Add Close-Ups (5 min)**
- Replace some mediums with close-ups for emphasis
- Use for emotional moments or key lines
- Don't overuseâ€”variety is key

**Step 4: Audio Smoothing (5 min)**
- Add J-cuts and L-cuts for natural flow
- Ensure no audio pops or clicks
- Dialogue should flow naturally

**Step 5: Final Polish (5 min)**
- Watch full sequence
- Trim any awkward pauses
- Ensure all cuts are motivated
- Check for continuity errors

**Adobe Premiere Techniques:**

**Keyboard Shortcuts for Speed:**
- `,` (comma): Insert edit
- `.` (period): Overwrite edit
- `Q` / `W`: Trim previous/next edit to playhead
- `Cmd+K`: Cut at playhead
- `Shift+Delete`: Ripple delete

**Matching Action:**
- Zoom in timeline for frame accuracy
- Find exact frame where action starts
- Cut just before or during movement
- Motion masks the edit

## Community Best Practices: Continuity Editing

### From Professional Editors

**1. "Coverage is King"**
- Shoot more angles than you think you need
- Wide, medium, close-up for every scene
- Gives options in editing room
- "You can't cut to a shot you didn't film"

**2. "Cut on Action"**
- Movement masks transitions
- Cut when character gestures, stands, turns
- Brain focuses on action, not edit
- Makes cuts invisible

**3. "Let the Scene Breathe"**
- Don't cut too quickly
- Hold on reactions
- Silence can be powerful
- Not every moment needs a cut

**4. "Audio Leads the Edit"**
- Start with audio-only edit (radio cut)
- Dialogue should flow naturally
- Then add visuals to support audio
- J-cuts and L-cuts are essential

**5. "Continuity Checklist"**
- Screen direction consistent?
- Eyelines match?
- Props in same positions?
- Lighting/color consistent?
- Audio levels matched?

### Continuity in Advertising

**Why Continuity Matters More in Ads:**

**1. Limited Time**
- 30-60 seconds total
- Can't afford viewer confusion
- Every second must be clear

**2. Product Focus**
- Product must be clearly shown
- Spatial relationships matter (size, context)
- Continuity ensures product is hero

**3. Professional Perception**
- Smooth editing = high quality brand
- Continuity errors = amateur/cheap
- Brands invest in polish

**Examples of Continuity in Ads:**
- Car commercials: Vehicle moves smoothly through space
- Product demos: Clear before/after transitions
- Testimonials: Interview continuity maintains credibility

## Theory Integration: Gestalt Principles in Practice

### How Gestalt Laws Enable Continuity Editing

**1. Closure Allows Ellipsis**
- Don't show every moment
- Viewer fills gaps mentally
- Example: Character reaches for door â†’ Cut to character inside room
- We don't need to see door opening

**2. Continuity Creates Flow**
- Movement continues across cuts
- Example: Character walks left to right â†’ Next shot continues left to right
- Brain perceives continuous motion

**3. Proximity Creates Relationships**
- Shots in sequence feel related
- Example: Product shot â†’ Happy customer
- Viewer assumes product caused happiness (Kuleshov Effect preview!)

**4. Similarity Maintains Context**
- Consistent lighting/color = same scene
- Consistent framing = same moment
- Changes signal time/location shift

### The Psychology of Invisible Editing

**Why Don't We Notice Cuts?**

**1. Attention and Saccades**
- Eyes naturally jump (saccades) 3-4 times per second
- Brain suppresses visual input during saccades
- Film cuts mimic natural eye movement
- Cuts during action = masked by attention shift

**2. Cognitive Load**
- Brain focused on story, not technique
- If story is engaging, we don't analyze editing
- Continuity errors only noticed if they break immersion

**3. Learned Convention**
- We've been trained by 100+ years of cinema
- Continuity editing is "language" we understand
- Shot-reverse-shot feels natural because we've seen it thousands of times

> *"Continuity editing is not naturalâ€”it is learned. But once learned, it becomes invisible. This is the editor's greatest trick."*
