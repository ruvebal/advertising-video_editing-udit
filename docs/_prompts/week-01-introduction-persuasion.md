## Context & Learning Goals

### Historical Foundation
- **Marshall McLuhan's "The Medium is the Message" (1964):** The form of media influences its impact on the audience, often as much as content does
- **Guy Debord's Society of the Spectacle (1967):** News, propaganda, advertising, and entertainment blend into an all-encompassing spectacle that shapes reality
- **Jean Kilbourne's critique:** Advertising sells not just products but concepts of love, beauty, success, sexuality, and normalcy

- Adobe Premiere Pro workspace navigation and interface
- Basic timeline editing and three-point editing
- Import/export fundamentals
- Essential keyboard shortcuts for efficiency
- Project organization and folder structure

### Critical Thinking Objectives
- How does editing make messages persuasive?
- Ethics of manipulation vs. persuasion
- Platform-specific considerations (TV vs. YouTube vs. TikTok)
- Unconscious influence of editing choices on viewers

### Week 1 Success Indicators
By the end of Week 1, students should be able to:
- Navigate Premiere Pro interface confidently
- Import media and create sequences

- Articulate how editing serves persuasion

### Screening & Analysis (45 minutes)

#### Example 1: Apple "1984" Super Bowl Commercial (60 seconds)

**Context:** Aired during 1984 Super Bowl, directed by Ridley Scott, introducing Macintosh computer

{% include video-player.html
   platform="youtube"
   id="ErwS24cBZPc"
   title="Apple 1984 Super Bowl Commercial"
   timestamps="0:00|Opening - Dystopian World,0:30|Heroine Appears,0:50|Chase Sequence,1:00|Hammer Throw & Explosion" %}

**Screening:** Play twice
- First viewing: Just watch and feel
- Second viewing: Analyze editing choices

**Analysis Points:**
- **Opening:** Dystopian gray world, marching workers (sets mood immediately)
- **Pacing:** Slow build with rhythmic cuts matching footsteps
- **Montage:** Intercutting between Big Brother screen and running heroine
- **Climax:** Rapid cuts as she throws hammer, slow-motion explosion
- **Resolution:** Simple text reveal of product
- **Duration:** Tells complete story in 60 seconds

**Editing Techniques:**
- Continuity editing for clarity (we follow heroine's journey)
- Montage for tension (cutting between threat and hero)
- Slow-motion for emphasis (explosion moment)
- Color grading (gray dystopia vs. colorful athlete)

**Persuasive Strategy:**
- Positions Apple as rebel against conformity (IBM = Big Brother)
- Emotional appeal: freedom, individuality, revolution
- Minimal product focus (only text at end) â€“ selling ideology

**Discussion:** "How does the editing make you *feel* about the product before you even see it?"

#### Example 2: Modern TikTok Viral Ad (15-30 seconds)

**Context:** Choose a recent viral TikTok ad (e.g., Duolingo, Ryanair, or creator-brand partnership)

**Screening:** Play 2-3 times

**Analysis Points:**
- **Hook:** First 2 seconds must stop the scroll (movement, text, surprise)
- **Format:** Vertical (9:16), fills phone screen
- **Pacing:** Extremely fast cuts (1-2 seconds per shot)
- **Text overlays:** Assumes sound might be off
- **Loop potential:** End connects to beginning for replay
- **Trend integration:** Uses popular sounds or meme formats

**Editing Techniques:**
- Jump cuts (no smooth transitions, pure energy)
- Text animations synced to beats
- Fast montage of product benefits
- Call-to-action in last frame

**Persuasive Strategy:**
- Entertainment first, advertising second (doesn't feel like ad)
- Authenticity over polish (relatable, not corporate)
- Engagement bait (encourages comments, shares)

**Discussion:** "How is this different from the Apple ad? What does the platform demand?"

#### Comparison Exercise (Group Discussion)

Create comparison chart on board:

| Element | TV (Apple 1984) | TikTok (Modern) |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Duration | 60 seconds | 15-30 seconds |
| Orientation | Horizontal | Vertical |
| Pacing | Builds slowly | Hooks immediately |
| Sound | Essential | Optional |
| Style | Cinematic | Authentic/Raw |
| Goal | Brand ideology | Engagement/Virality |

**Key Insight:** The *medium* (TV vs. TikTok) dictates the *editing approach*. McLuhan was right!

### Adobe Premiere Pro Workshop (50 minutes)

#### Part 1: Interface Tour (20 minutes)

**Launch Premiere Pro and create new project together**

**Four Main Panels:**

1. **Project Panel (Top Left):**
   - Where all media lives (footage, audio, graphics)
   - Organization: Create bins (folders) for structure
   - **Best Practice:** Create bins: RAW_FOOTAGE, AUDIO, GRAPHICS, SEQUENCES, EXPORTS

2. **Source Monitor (Middle Left):**
   - Preview clips before adding to timeline
   - Set In/Out points to select portions
   - **Keyboard shortcuts:** `I` (In point), `O` (Out point)

3. **Timeline (Bottom):**
   - Where you assemble your edit
   - Video tracks (V1, V2, V3...) stack vertically
   - Audio tracks (A1, A2, A3...) below
   - **Playhead:** Red line showing current frame

4. **Program Monitor (Middle Right):**
   - Preview your edited sequence
   - What the final video looks like
   - Export from here

**Demonstrate:**
- Import media (File > Import or drag-and-drop)
- Create sequence (Right-click footage > New Sequence from Clip)
- Add clip to timeline (drag or use Insert/Overwrite)
- Play/pause (Spacebar)

#### Part 3: Three-Point Editing Workflow (20 minutes)

**Professional editors use "three-point editing" for precision:**

**The Three Points:**
1. In point in Source monitor (where clip starts)
2. Out point in Source monitor (where clip ends)
3. In point on Timeline (where it goes)

**Two Ways to Add Clips:**
- **Insert (`,` comma key):** Pushes existing clips forward
- **Overwrite (`.` period key):** Replaces existing clips

**Demonstrate workflow:**
1. Open clip in Source monitor
2. Play through, mark In (`I`) where you want it to start
3. Mark Out (`O`) where you want it to end
4. Click on Timeline where you want it to go
5. Press `.` to Overwrite (or `,` to Insert)

**Why this matters:**
- Faster than dragging clips
- More precise
- Industry standard workflow
- Keeps hands on keyboard

**Student practice (10 minutes):**
- Provide 5 short clips
- Use three-point editing to assemble them in sequence
- Experiment with Insert vs. Overwrite

### Practical Exercise: "The Power of Pacing" (50 minutes)

#### Exercise Overview

**Objective:** Edit the same dialogue scene two ways to viscerally feel how pacing changes meaning and emotional impact.

**Materials Provided:**
- 2-minute dialogue scene with multiple camera angles
- Characters A and B having a conversation
- Coverage: Wide shot, Medium shots (both characters), Close-ups (both characters)

**Task:** Create TWO versions of a 30-second excerpt:

**Version 1: Slow & Contemplative**
- Long takes (5-8 seconds per shot)
- Minimal cuts (maybe 4-6 total)
- Let moments breathe
- Creates: intimacy, thoughtfulness, drama, weight

**Version 2: Fast & Energetic**
- Rapid cuts (1-3 seconds per shot)
- Quick shot-reverse-shot (12-15 cuts)
- Dynamic pacing
- Creates: tension, excitement, urgency, anxiety

#### Step-by-Step Instructions (Demonstrate, then students do)

**Step 1: Create Sequences (5 minutes)**
- Right-click any footage clip
- Select "New Sequence from Clip" (ensures correct settings)
- Name it: "01_SLOW_PACING_v01"
- Duplicate sequence: "02_FAST_PACING_v01"

**Step 2: Watch All Coverage (5 minutes)**
- Review all angles in Source monitor
- Understand the conversation flow
- Note which angles show which emotions best

**Step 3: Edit Version 1 - Slow (15 minutes)**

**Approach:**
- Start with Wide shot (establish space, 6-8 seconds)
- Cut to Medium of Character A (full line of dialogue, 5-7 seconds)
- Cut to Medium of Character B (response, 5-7 seconds)
- Cut to Close-up of A (emotional moment, hold 6 seconds)
- End on Wide (resolution, 4-5 seconds)

**Adobe Techniques to Demonstrate:**
- **Ripple Edit Tool (`B`):** Trim clip and automatically close gap
- **Rolling Edit Tool (`N`):** Adjust cut point between two clips without changing duration
- Drag edges of clips to extend/shorten
- Watch Program monitor to feel the pacing

**Step 4: Edit Version 2 - Fast (15 minutes)**

**Approach:**
- Start with Wide (establish, 2 seconds)
- Rapid shot-reverse-shot between Close-ups
- Cut every 1-2 seconds, sometimes mid-sentence
- Intercutting reactions while other person speaks
- Build rhythm and energy
- End abruptly on reaction

**Adobe Techniques:**
- Use Razor tool (`C`) to make quick cuts
- Or use `Cmd+K` to cut at playhead
- Ripple delete (`Shift+Del`) to remove unwanted sections
- Fine-tune with frame-by-frame navigation (arrow keys)

**Step 5: Compare & Reflect (10 minutes)**
- Play both versions back-to-back
- Notice how different they feel
- Same footage, same dialogue, completely different emotional impact

### Community Best Practices (Integrated Throughout)

**From Professional Editors:**

1. **"Edit with your ears first"**
   - Audio continuity guides visual cuts
   - If audio sounds smooth, visual cut will feel smooth
   - Listen for natural pauses to place cuts

2. **"Cut on movement"**
   - Motion masks the transition
   - Cut when character gestures, turns head, stands up
   - Eye won't notice the cut during movement

3. **"When in doubt, cut it out"**
   - Tighten for impact
   - Every frame should serve the story
   - If a shot doesn't add value, remove it

4. **"Save versions"**
   - Duplicate sequences before major changes
   - Name clearly: v01, v02, v03
   - Never lose your work to experimentation

## Theory Integration Points

### Concepts to Emphasize Throughout Week 1

#### 1. Editing as Rhetoric
- Every cut is an argument
- Sequence creates meaning (what comes first shapes what comes next)
- Omission is as powerful as inclusion (what you DON'T show matters)
- The editor is making thousands of micro-arguments

#### 2. Platform Awareness
Understanding how distribution channel shapes editing approach:

**Television (Traditional):**
- 30-second spots, horizontal 16:9
- Sound-on (viewers expect audio)
- Captive audience during commercial break
- High production value expected
- Can build slowly (first 5 seconds less critical)

**YouTube:**
- 6-60 seconds (skippable after 5 seconds)
- Horizontal, but also square for mobile
- Must hook in first 5 seconds or lose viewer
- Thumbnail + title critical for click
- Longer form acceptable if engaging

**TikTok:**
- 15-60 seconds optimal
- Vertical 9:16 (fills phone screen)
- Sound-optional (captions essential)
- Hook in first 2 seconds (stop the scroll)
- Loop-friendly (end connects to beginning)
- Trend integration (sounds, formats, memes)

**Instagram:**
- Feed: Square 1:1 or portrait 4:5
- Stories: Vertical 9:16, 15 seconds per slide
- Reels: Vertical 9:16, 15-90 seconds
- Sound-optional (many browse muted)
- First frame is thumbnail (make it count)

#### 3. Ethical Considerations

**The Spectrum of Persuasion:**
- **Information:** Presenting facts clearly
- **Persuasion:** Making compelling argument
- **Manipulation:** Exploiting psychological vulnerabilities
- **Deception:** Presenting falsehoods

**Questions to Ask:**
- Am I being truthful in my representation?
- Am I reinforcing harmful stereotypes?
- Am I exploiting insecurities or fears?
- Would I be proud to show this to diverse audiences?
- Is my persuasion ethical or manipulative?

**Feminist Critique (Kilbourne):**
- Objectification of bodies (especially women)
- Unrealistic beauty standards
- Gender role stereotyping
- Sexual exploitation for product sales

**Anti-Consumerist Critique (Klein):**
- Creating false needs
- Branding as identity replacement
- Corporate co-opting of social movements
- Environmental impact of consumption

## Technical Deep-Dive: Adobe Premiere Pro Fundamentals

### 1. Project Setup Best Practices

**Sequence Settings (Choose based on source footage):**

```
CINEMATIC (Film-like):
- Resolution: 1920x1080 (Full HD)
- Frame Rate: 23.976 fps
- Pixel Aspect Ratio: Square
- Audio: 48kHz, 16-bit

BROADCAST (TV/Professional):
- Resolution: 1920x1080 (Full HD)
- Frame Rate: 29.97 fps
- Pixel Aspect Ratio: Square
- Audio: 48kHz, 16-bit

SOCIAL MEDIA VERTICAL:
- Resolution: 1080x1920 (9:16)
- Frame Rate: 30 fps
- Pixel Aspect Ratio: Square
- Audio: 48kHz, 16-bit

4K (High-end):
- Resolution: 3840x2160
- Frame Rate: 23.976 or 29.97 fps
- Pixel Aspect Ratio: Square
- Audio: 48kHz, 24-bit
```

**Pro Tip:** Use "New Sequence from Clip" to automatically match source footage settings

### 2. Import Strategies

**Method 1: Media Browser**
- Advantage: Preview clips before importing
- Navigate to folder in Media Browser panel
- Scrub through clips to see content
- Import only what you need

**Method 2: Import Dialog (Cmd+I)**
- Advantage: Batch import entire folders
- Can import sequences, projects, images, audio
- Faster for large amounts of media

**Ingest Options:**
- **Copy to project location:** Safer (all media in one place), uses more storage
- **Link to original:** Saves space, but risky if original files move
- **Create proxies on import:** For 4K footage, create lower-res editing copies

### 3. Workspace Customization

**Recommended Workspaces:**
- **Editing:** Default, good for most work
- **Color:** For color grading (Lumetri panel prominent)
- **Audio:** For sound mixing (Essential Sound panel)
- **Graphics:** For titles and motion graphics

**Customize your own:**
- Drag panels to resize
- Save workspace: Window > Workspaces > Save as New Workspace
- Reset if things get messy: Window > Workspaces > Reset to Saved Layout

### 4. Essential Effects & Transitions

**The 99% Rule: Use Straight Cuts**

Most professional editing uses simple cuts. Transitions should be rare and purposeful.

**When to Use Transitions:**

**Cross Dissolve:**
- Time passage ("Later that day...")
- Dreamy/romantic mood
- Softening harsh cut
- Montage sequences

**Dip to Black/White:**
- Chapter breaks
- Dramatic pause
- End of sequence
- Death/serious moment

**AVOID:**
- Star wipes, page peels, fancy effects
- These scream "amateur" in professional work
- Exception: Intentional retro/campy style

<figure class="quote">
<blockquote>
The best transition is no transitionâ€”the cut that serves the story invisibly.
</blockquote>
<figcaption>
&mdash; Tao of the Video Editor
</figcaption>
</figure>

**Audio Transitions:**
- **Constant Power Crossfade:** Default, good for most
- **Exponential Fade:** Better for music fades
- **J-Cut:** Audio leads video (hear next scene before seeing it)
- **L-Cut:** Video leads audio (see next scene while hearing previous)

### 5. Playback Optimization

**If playback is choppy:**
1. Lower playback resolution (click wrench icon in Program monitor)
   - Set to 1/2 or 1/4 for smooth playback
   - Doesn't affect export quality
2. Render In to Out (`Enter` key)
   - Red line above timeline = needs rendering
   - Yellow = may need rendering
   - Green = rendered, smooth playback
3. Use proxy workflow for 4K footage
   - Create low-res copies for editing
   - Auto-switch to high-res on export

**Performance Tips:**
- Close other applications
- Clear media cache: Preferences > Media Cache > Clean
- Use SSD for media storage if possible
- Increase RAM allocation: Preferences > Memory

### Common Student Challenges

**Challenge 1: Premiere Pro interface overwhelming**
- **Solution:** Focus on essential tools only (don't show every panel)
- Reassure: "You'll learn more tools as needed, not all at once"
- Provide cheat sheet of essential shortcuts

**Challenge 2: Difficulty seeing pacing differences**
- **Solution:** Play examples side-by-side
- Exaggerate the difference (make slow VERY slow, fast VERY fast)
- Use music to emphasize rhythm

**Challenge 3: Technical issues (crashes, missing files, export errors)**
- **Solution:** Have backup computers available
- Create troubleshooting guide handout
- Build in buffer time for tech support
- Remind students: Save often (Cmd+S)!

**Challenge 4: Students finish at different speeds**
- **Solution:** Have extension exercises ready
  - "Try a third version: medium pacing"
  - "Add music to one version"
  - "Experiment with color grading"
- Pair fast finishers with those who need help

### Facilitation Tips

**Create Safe Learning Environment:**
- Emphasize: "Mistakes are learning opportunities"
- Model vulnerability: "I still make mistakes after years of editing"
- Celebrate experimentation over perfection
- No judgment in critiqueâ€”only constructive feedback

**Encourage Active Participation:**
- Cold call gently: "What do you think, [name]?"
- Use think-pair-share for discussions
- Move around room during lab time
- Check in with quiet students individually

**Model Enthusiasm:**
- Share your passion for editing
- Tell stories about professional experiences
- Show excitement about student discoveries
- Celebrate "happy accidents" in editing

**Manage Time:**
- Use timer for exercises (visible to students)
- Give 5-minute and 1-minute warnings
- Be flexible but keep moving forward
- Save detailed questions for office hours

### Accessibility Considerations

- Provide captions for all video examples
- Offer alternative formats for readings (audio, large print)
- Ensure software is accessible (screen reader compatible)
- Allow extra time for students who need it
- Provide notes/slides before class for review

### Differentiation Strategies

**For Advanced Students:**
- Challenge them to add music synced to cuts
- Experiment with color grading for mood
- Try more complex shot sequences
- Mentor peers who are struggling

**For Struggling Students:**
- Simplify exercise: Just create one version
- Provide more structured step-by-step guide
- Pair with supportive peer
- Offer office hours for one-on-one help
- Focus on core concepts, not perfection
